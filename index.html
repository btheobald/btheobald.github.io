SPINE - FPGA Based Hardware Acceleration

‹ ›

    An investigation into low-cost SoC hardware acceleration.

    Inspired by The University of Tokyo's GRAPE (GRAvity PipE) projects which are designed to accelerate the N-Body problem.

    SPINE intends to demonstrate the performance improvement that can be obtained by tightly coupling a CPU with custom acceleration logic for a variety of computational problems.

    The system is designed around a DE1-SoC, which uses a 85k LE Cyclone V FPGA and a dual-core ARM Cortex-A9 Hard Processor System. The HPS runs Linux and performs time-integration of the simulation.

    The FPGA fabric is purely dedicated to a number of pipelines used to calculate of the force bettween particles. Simulation data is transfered via FTP to a secondary computer responsible for rendering.

    "Coulomb" - GX80 Battery Grip

    ‹ ›

      4000mAh+ Battery Grip for a MFT Camera.

      I tend to take a lot of photos when I'm hiking and I currently use a USB battery pack to recharge it while out and about. On longer trips though I prefer to keep this for charging my phone, plus the camera cannot be used while charging.

      My solution is an integrated battery pack which connects to a custom adapter through the battery door. This design holds two 18650, 20700 or 21700 cells, providing more than 4000mAh of capacity at a similar cost of a first party 1000mAh battery.

      The product will feature an integrated battery management system along with status LEDs.

      Autodesk Fusion360 was used for 3D CAD models, KiCAD for circuit design and PCB layout.

      Status: Casing and Electronics Development.


      "Atom" - High Brightness LED Driver

      ‹ ›

        6.2W Dual LED String Driver

        Atom was the second stage of development for the LED driver used in project Aim, acheiving 62% size reduction over the previous driver board prototype (Project London, Final Image).

        The device made use of two TI TPS61165 high brightness white LED drivers in tandem with a ATTiny10 MCU for control. Each strip used a different colour temperature LED to provide the user with the choice of lower brightness reading (3000k) or higher brightness working light (3000k + 6000k).

        The LED board itself was also a significant point of investigation, as considerations needed to be made for thermal management. Approximately 5.5W is dissipated by the LEDs at 100% brightness. Vias reduce thermal resistance to the reverse of the board. This was attached to a heat spreader using thermal epoxy. The heat spreader then made contact with the casing.

        The design for the electronics was produced using KiCAD. No credit was avaliable on the course for electronics, but it was invaluable nontheless.

          "X302" Development Board, this used a STM32F302 ARM Cortex M4 on a custom PCB with various components, such as a coin cell holder and Micro-SD card slot. The PCB was sized to allow it to fit inside a handheld case for rapid prototyping of a specialist product.

          "Aim" - Desk Lamp

          ‹ ›

            Articulating 600 Lumen LED Lamp

            My most complex mechanical design by far, realised for my Product Design A2 project. The design was produced using Autodesk Inventor and all metal parts were machined by hand.

            The main mantra while designing the unit was modularity to introduce the idea of a 'design ecosystem'. Various designs and construction methods were tested for the hinges, but 3D printing was used as it enabled complex and accurate designs to be produced.

            Modularity also alowed the unit to be completely disassembled. The lamp can be packed into a box which can fit through a letter box.

            The electronics which drive the dual LED strings were completely custom designed and fit entirely within the upper module containing the interface buttons. (See Project - Atom)


            "Exo" - N-Body Simulator

            ‹ ›

              Multithreaded C++/OpenGL N-Body Simulator

              When starting out my A-Level project for Computing, I set myself the challenge of understanding more than my course required.

              Combining my knowledge of programming and physics, I designed from the ground up a simulator that would test the limits of performance of brute force algorithms, multithreaded data management and introduce me to graphics programming.

              The project was a success, and the end result is a N-Body simulator capable of handling 2000 bodies at 30FPS, this is one of the most complex programming projects I have ever undertaken by myself.

              Source code for this project is public on github.

                System Design and Casing for a Smart Meter

                At the start of 2019 I participated in a group design project for my degree course, focused on the design and construction of a smart meter to interface with a NI LabView simulation.

                "Cram" - Integrated Circuit Design Project

                ‹ ›

                  Layout of Ring Oscillator and Multiplier

                  The first group design project of my second year of study focused on the design and layout of several circuits to be produced on an AMS (Austria Microsystems) 350nm process and then tested at the end of the second semester.

                  There were 4 major components constructed, a 18MHz ring oscillator, a serial sequence detector, a 4-bit full-adder and a 7-bit unsigned multiplier.

                  I was primarily responsible for the layout and verification of the oscillator and the multiplier. The main challenge was making the multiplier as compact as possible but also easily expandable in the event that more space was avaliable.

                  This was done by building multiplier cells using a slightly different full-adder design which made use of a multiplexer and 2 XOR gates, rather than the traditional adder design which required more area. This was complemented by a desgin which could be arrayed with minimal manual connections required.
